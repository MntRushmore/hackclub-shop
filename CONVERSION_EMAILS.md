# Parent campaign: email + text copy

Drives parents to **shop.hackclub.com**. The link now renders a branded preview
card (the OG image we built), so the link itself sells before they even click.

**Voice:** warm, proud, parent to parent. Not salesy, not teen slang. The product
is the *feeling* (pride in your kid + supporting the cause), not the shirt.
**One CTA per send.** No em dashes anywhere, per house style.

---

## Strategy in one paragraph

A parent on this list already knows Hack Club (their kid is in it, or they met us
on the Zoom call). They are not deciding *whether Hack Club is good*. They are
deciding *whether to act right now*. So the job of the email is not to explain
Hack Club. It is to (1) give them a feeling worth acting on ("I'm proud of my kid
and I can show it"), (2) make the ask tiny and concrete ("see the collection"),
and (3) remove the two hesitations parents have about buying online for a cause:
"is this legit / where does the money go" (answer: 501(c)(3), all proceeds to
teenagers) and "is it a hassle" (answer: ships to your door, secure checkout).

---

## Wave 1: the introduction (send to the whole list)

Goal: open + click. Lead with pride, not product.

**Subject line A/B test (pick the winner for Wave 2 timing):**
- A: `You raised a Hack Clubber.`
- B: `Something for the maker in your house`
- C: `For the parent who's quietly very proud`

**Preview text (the gray line after the subject):**
`Wear it proudly. Every purchase supports teenagers at Hack Club.`

**Body:**

> **You raised a Hack Clubber.**
>
> Somewhere right now, or last Tuesday at midnight, a kid was deep in a project
> nobody assigned them. A game. A website. A little robot.
> Something they decided the world should have, and then started building.
>
> That is a Hack Clubber. And you are the person who made room for it.
>
> We made a small collection for the people who love them: apparel and goods you
> can actually wear and use, designed so you can say out loud what you have
> probably been feeling quietly for a while.
>
> Every single purchase goes back to the teenagers. Hack Club is a 501(c)(3)
> nonprofit, and proceeds fund the hackathons, clubs, and free programs that give
> a kid somewhere to build.
>
> **[ See the collection ]**  → shop.hackclub.com
>
> Ships to your door with tracking. Secure checkout. Takes about two minutes.
>
> With gratitude,
> The Hack Club team

**Why this works:**
- Opens with *their* memory of their kid, not our brand. Earns the next line.
- "You are the person who made room for it" is the emotional hook. That is what
  they are buying.
- The cause + 501(c)(3) line answers "where does my money go" before they ask.
- The CTA is soft ("see the collection," not "buy now") because Wave 1's job is
  the click, not the close. The product page does the closing now.

## Wave 2: the nudge (send 4 to 6 days later, ONLY to non-purchasers)

Segment out anyone who already bought. Wave 2 is for openers-who-didn't-buy and
non-openers. Shorter, more concrete, gentle urgency from *meaning*, not fake
scarcity.

**Subject line (match to behavior if your tool allows):**
- For non-openers (resend): `In case you missed it: you raised a Hack Clubber`
- For openers-who-didn't-buy: `Still thinking about it? Here's where it goes.`

**Preview text:**
`A two-minute way to back the next thing a kid builds.`

**Body:**

> **A quick, honest note about where the money goes.**
>
> Last week we shared the Hack Club Shop. If you have been meaning to take a look,
> here is the part worth knowing:
>
> There is no company pocketing the margin. Hack Club is a 501(c)(3) nonprofit,
> and what you spend here funds real things for teenagers:
>
> - **Hackathons** where kids build all weekend with real mentors, most free to attend
> - **Clubs** in high schools around the world
> - **Free tools and hardware** that put making within reach of any kid who wants it
>
> So when you wear the shirt, you are wearing something you helped pay for, for a
> kid who is not yours but might as well be.
>
> **[ Find something you'll be proud to wear ]**  → shop.hackclub.com
>
> Ships to your door. Secure checkout. And if you have a question first, just
> reply to this email or write shop@hackclub.com.

**Why this works:**
- Names the exact hesitation ("where does the money go") and answers it with
  specifics, not slogans. Parents trust specifics.
- "a kid who is not yours but might as well be" widens the gift from "my kid" to
  "the cause" without losing the warmth.
- Offers a human reply path (shop@hackclub.com). For a nonprofit ask, a reachable
  human is a conversion tool, not just support.

## Forwardable text message (for the Zoom-call parents + word of mouth)

Short enough to paste into iMessage/WhatsApp. The link preview does the visual
work, so the text just needs warmth and a reason.

> Hack Club (the thing my kid is obsessed with) made a little shop. All of it
> goes back to the teenagers. Thought of you. shop.hackclub.com

Alt, for a parent sharing to another parent:

> If you've got a kid who can't stop building, this one's for you. Every purchase
> funds Hack Club's free programs for teens. shop.hackclub.com

---

## Send mechanics (recommendations, not built)

- **From name:** a person, not "Hack Club Shop." e.g. "Zach at Hack Club." Parent
  emails from a human get opened.
- **Wave 1 timing:** weekday morning (Tue to Thu, 8 to 10am local). Parents read
  email with coffee.
- **Wave 2:** 4 to 6 days after Wave 1, suppress purchasers, send early evening.
- **Track:** open rate (subject test), click rate (body/CTA), and the real number
  that matters, **link → checkout-started → paid**. If clicks are high but paid is
  low, the problem is on-site (we just hardened that path). If clicks are low, the
  problem is the subject line.
- **One link only.** Every email points to shop.hackclub.com. No competing CTAs.
